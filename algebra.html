    Subsubsection 1.1.1 What is Algebra?

    In its most general form, algebra is the study of mathematical symbols and the rules for manipulating these symbols. It is a unifying thread of almost all of mathematics. (‚Ä¶) The more basic parts of algebra are called elementary algebra, the more abstract parts are called abstract algebra or modern algebra. Elementary algebra is essential for any study of mathematics, science, or engineering, as well as such applications as medicine and economics. Abstract algebra is a major area in advanced mathematics, studied primarily by professional mathematicians.     Subsubsection 1.1.2 The Building Blocks of Algebra

    The main building blocks of algebra are variables, expressions and equations.
    Variables.
    Variables can have different interpretations depending on the situation:
    • In an equation (see below) a variable may stand for an unknown number:
      \begin{equation*} x+2=5 \end{equation*}
    • A variable may be used to describe a general situation: If a car is going 5 m/s then after \(t\) seconds it will have traveled \(s=5\cdot t\) meters.
    • Variables can describe relationships between quantities that can vary: If \(c\) is the circumference of a circle and \(d\) is its diameter, then the ratio \(\frac{c}{d}\) is always constant and \(\frac{c}{d}=\pi\text{.}\)
    • Variables can be used to describe general rules (or theorems) of mathematics: The operation addition is commutative i.e. \(a+b=b+a\text{.}\)
    Expressions.
    For example,
    • Added terms are simplified using coefficients. For example, \(x + x + x\) can be simplified as \(3\cdot x\) (where 3 is the coefficient).
    • Multiplied terms are simplified using exponents. For example, \(x \cdot x \cdot x\) is represented as \(x^3\)
    • Like terms are added together, for example, \(2\cdot x^2 + 3\cdot a\cdot b - x^2 + a\cdot b\) is written as \(x^2 + 4\cdot a\cdot b\text{,}\) because the terms containing \(x^2\) are added together, and, the terms containing \(a\cdot b\) are added together.
    • Brackets can be "multiplied out", using distributivity. For example, \(x\cdot (2x + 3)\) can be written as \((x \cdot 2x) + (x \cdot 3)\) which can be written as \(2x^2 + 3x\text{.}\)
    • Expressions can be factored. For example, \(6x^5 + 3x^2\text{,}\) by dividing both terms by \(3x^2\) can be written as \(3x^2\cdot (2x^3 + 1)\)

    • Some equations are true for all values of the involved variables: The above example of the commutativity of addition states that \(a+b=b+a\) for all values of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
    • Some equations are only true for certain values: \(x^2-1=0\) is only true if \(x=1\) or if \(x=-1\text{.}\)

    Subsection 1.2 Numbers and Expressions

    The most fundamental building blocks in mathematics are numbers and the operations that can be performed on them. Algebra, like arithmetic, involves performing operations such as addition, subtraction, multiplication and division on numbers. In arithmetic, we are performing operations on known, specific, numbers (e.g. \(5 + 3 = 8\)). However, in algebra we often deal with operations on unknown numbers represented by variables usually symbolized by a letter (e .g. \(\frac{a+b}{c}=\frac{a}{c}+\frac{b}{c}\)). The use of variables gives us the power to write general statements indicating relationships between numbers.

    Subsubsection 1.2.1 Real Numbers

    A real number is any number that can be represented by a point on the real number line.
    Each point on the real number line corresponds to one and only one real number, and each real number corresponds to one and only one point on the real number line. This kind of relationship is called a one-to-one correspondence. The number associated with a point on the real number line is called the coordinate of the point.

    Subsubsection 1.2.4 Working with Fractions

    A fraction \(\dfrac{a}{b}\) ¬†is simply an alternative way of writing the division \(a:b\text{.}\) If you remember the following, then you should be set:
    • In a fraction the upper number (or expression) is called the numerator.
    • The lower number (or expression) is called the denominator.
    • A fraction can be simplified if and only if the numerator and denominator share a common factor (e.g. \(\frac{4}{12}=\frac{4\cdot 1}{4\cdot 3}=\frac{1}{3}\)).
    • The minus sign of a negative fraction can be in many different places:
      \begin{equation*} =-\frac{a}{b}=\frac{-a}{b}=\frac{a}{-b}=(-1)\cdot\frac{a}{b}\ . \end{equation*}
    • Fractions can be added and subtracted only if they have a common denominator:
      \begin{equation*} \frac{a+c}{2b^2}+\frac{a-c}{2b^2}=\frac{2a}{2b^2}=\frac{a}{b^2}\ . \end{equation*}
    • Sometimes it is useful to split a fraction in multiple summands:
      \begin{equation*} \frac{a+b}{c}=\frac{a}{c}+\frac{b}{c}\ . \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 1.2.5 Solving Word Problems

    A classic math word problem consists of two steps:
    • Translate the problem into an equation.
    • Solve the equation.
    As we gather more experience we will see that it is often necessary to add a third step to this process:
    • Assess the solution: Do the numbers make sense? What consequences does this result have? etc.
    Even further along we will add an additional step at the very beginning:
    • Create a model that describes a given situation.
    I hope you‚Äôll agree with me that the second step is the most boring one here. As a result the other steps will become more and more prominent as we progress.
    Translate the following into an equation and solve.
    1. Four times a number is \(48\text{.}\)
    2. A number doubled and reduced by \(12\) equals \(10\text{.}\)
    3. The sum of a number and its half equals \(33\text{.}\)
    To do a rope trick, a magician needs to cut a piece of rope so that one piece is one-third the length of the other piece. If she begins with a \(\frac{8}{3}\)ft rope, what lengths will the two pieces of rope be?
    Of three consecutive even integers, the sum of the smallest two integers is equal to 6 less than the largest. Find the integers.
    Pat averages a rate of 11 mph on his bike. One day he rode for 45 min (\(\frac{3}{4}\)hr) and then got a flat tire and had to walk back home. He walked the same path that he rode and it took him 2 hr. What was his average rate walking?
    Sharyn invests $2000 more in an account that earns 9% simple interest than she invests in an account that earns 6% simple interest. How much did she invest in each account if her total interest is $405 after 1 year?
    In 2003, approximately 7.2 million men were in college in the United States.This represents an 8% increase over the number of men in college in 2000. Approximately how many men were in college in 2000? (Round to the nearest tenth of a million.)
    In 2002, there were 17‚Äô430 deaths due to alcohol related accidents in the United States. This was a 5% increase over the number of alcohol-related deaths in 1999. How many such deaths were there in 1999?
    Which is a better fit, a square peg in a round hole or a round peg in a square hole?
    Take any two digit number, reverse its digits, and subtract the smaller number from the larger. Do you ever end up with a prime number?
    How many numbers from 1 to 100 can you make, using the digits 2, 0, 2, 3 exactly once. You can use any operations you want (addition, multiplication, powers, roots, etc.)
